War Thunder sorts every penetrator into two damage families, and armour reacts to each one differently. **Kinetic energy (KE)** rounds — solid-shot AP, APDS and <Term id="APFSDS">APFSDS</Term> — punch through with mass and velocity. **Chemical energy (CE)** rounds — <Term id="HEATFS">HEATFS</Term> and HESH — burn or squash their way in. Knowing which family you are firing, and which the enemy is firing at you, is the whole game when you read an armour layout.

## Steel: RHA, cast, and high-hardness

The baseline material is rolled homogeneous armour (RHA). Every other material carries an **armour modifier** that scales its effective thickness up or down relative to RHA. Cast homogeneous armour (CHA) is weaker per millimetre than rolled steel, so a cast turret of a given thickness protects less than a rolled plate of the same thickness. High-hardness rolled armour (HHRA) sits above standard RHA, squeezing more protection out of each millimetre.

The game combines material and slope in one calculation. Effective line-of-sight thickness is the plate thickness multiplied by its material modifier, divided by the cosine of the impact angle. That is why a thin, well-sloped plate can out-protect a thick vertical one, and why angling your hull matters as much as the steel behind it.

<Callout type="warning">Exact modifier figures for RHA, CHA, HHRA, screens, composite and each ERA model are rebalanced per patch and were read from a single source snapshot. Verify any specific number directly on the live wiki for Update Heavy Cavalry (2.57) before relying on it.</Callout>

## Spaced armour

Spaced armour places an outer screen ahead of the main hull with an air gap between them. Against CE rounds, the gap gives a shaped-charge jet room to spread and lose focus before it reaches the base armour. Against KE rounds, the first layer can shatter or destabilise an incoming penetrator. One exception: depleted-uranium penetrators are noted as more durable and resist being broken up by screen layers, whereas more brittle materials such as tungsten are more affected. In-game, screen and gap elements have their own modifiers — composite anti-HEAT screens, for example, give strong CE protection but little against solid kinetic shot.

## Composite and NERA

Composite armour is modelled as a stack of different materials layered together, offering steel-comparable protection at far lower mass. In-game composite blocks can contain NERA (non-explosive reactive armour) elements — bulging sandwich plates that deform on impact to disrupt a jet or penetrator. These NERA elements are far more effective against CE than against KE.

## ERA

Explosive reactive armour is modelled as metal containers holding an explosive layer between plates. On impact the explosive detonates and drives the plates sideways across the incoming jet, forcing it to cut through moving metal and lose penetration.

- **Strong against CE**, much weaker against KE.
- Effectiveness varies widely by generation — the wiki lists distinct values for models such as Blazer, Contact-1, Contact-5 and Relict.
- **One-time use**: a detonated tile leaves a gap, so a second round on the same spot meets only base armour.

The counter is tandem-charge ammunition: a precursor charge sets off the reactive tile so the main charge reaches the hull.

## Active protection systems

APS comes in two flavours. **Hard-kill** systems use sensors (such as Doppler radar) and a launcher that fires a directed shrapnel pattern to physically destroy the threat before impact. Early systems intercept slower threats like <Term id="ATGM">ATGMs</Term> and HEAT shells late in flight; modern ones can defeat high-velocity APFSDS at range. Coverage is directional — Drozd, for example, follows the turret and gives no rear protection. The interface shows your detection sector and remaining counter-munitions at lower-left, and you can resupply them at capture points.

**Soft-kill / electro-optical** systems use light and IR emitters to jam missile guidance rather than destroy the round. They work against <Term id="SACLOS">SACLOS</Term> missiles lacking ECCM, but do nothing against MCLOS, laser-beam-riding, TV-guided, or ECCM-equipped SACLOS missiles.

| Type | Hard-kill | Soft-kill |
|---|---|---|
| Method | Destroys the projectile | Jams missile guidance |
| Examples | Drozd, Trophy, Iron Fist, AMAP-ADS | Shtora-1, MUSS, AN/VLQ-8A, JD-3, EIREL |

The first hard-kill vehicles arrived in the Wind of Change update: the T-55AMD-1 (Drozd), Challenger Black Night (Iron Fist) and Merkava Mk.4M (Trophy).

## Real-world note

The composite and ERA technologies modelled here trace back to a real arms race: composite armour was developed to stop shaped charges capable of boring through roughly 400 mm of steel at far lower weight, while reactive armour answered the proliferation of man-portable HEAT weapons. The game's two-family KE/CE model is a simplification of those real interactions, so treat the in-game behaviour as a balanced approximation rather than ballistics fact.
