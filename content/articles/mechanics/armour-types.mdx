Armour in War Thunder is not a single number. The game treats a plate by its material,
its construction, and the kind of threat hitting it — which is why two tanks with the same
"thickness" on paper can behave completely differently when shot. The most important split is
between **kinetic** threats (solid shot and fin-stabilised darts) and **chemical** ones (shaped
charges and squashing rounds). Several armour types are good against one and poor against the
other.

## Rolled and cast homogeneous armour

The baseline is **rolled homogeneous armour (RHA)** — a single steel plate treated as the
reference standard, so penetration values are usually quoted in "millimetres of RHA." **Cast**
armour, used for rounded turrets and hulls, is modelled as slightly less effective than rolled
steel of the same thickness, trading a little protection for shapes that are easy to manufacture
and naturally well-angled.

For both, angle matters as much as thickness: a sloped plate presents more material along the
shot's line of sight and can deflect rounds entirely. That interaction is covered in detail in
the penetration article.

## Spaced armour

**Spaced armour** is two or more thinner plates with a gap between them rather than one thick
slab. The gap is cheap protection against chemical rounds in particular: a shaped charge's jet
begins to disperse after it punches the outer skin, so it arrives at the main plate with less
of its penetrating power intact. Thin spaced plates and skirts can also strip the cap off some
kinetic rounds or start a ricochet.

## Composite armour

**Composite** armour sandwiches materials such as ceramics between steel layers. It is
deliberately weighted toward defeating shaped charges and other chemical rounds, while still
adding meaningful protection against kinetic threats. In game terms, a composite array can carry
separate effectiveness against kinetic and chemical attacks, so a frontal package that shrugs off
a HEAT round may give up more ground to a fast APFSDS dart.

<Callout type="tip">
When you see a modern tank's "thick" frontal armour, check what kind of round you are firing.
Composite and spaced packages are tuned against chemical jets — a kinetic dart often does better
against them than a shaped charge does.
</Callout>

## Reactive and bulging armour

**Explosive reactive armour (ERA)** is a layer of bricks containing an explosive filler. When a
round strikes, the brick detonates outward, disrupting a shaped-charge jet and, on heavier modern
types, shearing or destabilising kinetic darts as well. **NERA** (non-energetic / "bulging"
armour) achieves a similar disruption without an explosive charge, using plates that flex and
move to upset the incoming penetrator. Both are add-on or built-in layers stacked in front of the
main armour, and both are far more useful against chemical rounds than against the heaviest
kinetic darts.

## Active protection systems

At the top of the tree, some vehicles carry an **active protection system (APS)**. Rather than
absorbing a hit, an APS tries to defeat the threat before it arrives — soft-kill systems try to
spoil a missile's guidance, while hard-kill systems fire an interceptor at the incoming round.
APS is most relevant against missiles and slower chemical threats and does nothing to stop a
gun-fired kinetic dart.

## Reading protection in practice

The takeaway is to think in matchups, not single numbers. A spaced-and-composite hull is built to
beat chemical rounds; bring kinetic. A lightly built but well-angled plate may bounce a dart that
arrives at a shallow angle; change your aim point or close the distance. The protection analysis
view in the hangar lets you probe a specific vehicle's armour against a specific round, which is
the most reliable way to learn where it is actually weak.

<Callout type="warning">
Exact armour values and the effectiveness of specific composite and ERA packages change between
patches. Treat the mechanics here as the durable model and confirm any specific figure against the
current in-game protection analysis for the vehicle you are studying.
</Callout>
