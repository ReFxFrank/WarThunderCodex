War Thunder offers three main PvP modes that climb a ladder of complexity and realism: Arcade Battles (AB), Realistic Battles (RB), and Simulator Battles (SB). Each applies to aviation, helicopters, ground vehicles, and naval forces. The official FAQ frames Arcade as the simplified, fast entry point for newcomers, while Realistic and Simulator are built for players chasing maximum historical accuracy. The label is the same across vehicle types, but the actual rules shift depending on whether you are flying, driving, or sailing.

## Arcade: assists on, friction off

Arcade hands you the most help. In the air, a lead marker shows where an enemy will be when your rounds arrive if it holds course, there are no stalling mechanics, and abrupt maneuvers cannot trigger spins or structural failure. On the ground, an aim assistant projects the impact point and a colored penetration indicator (red/yellow/green) estimates whether you will punch through, plus a lead marker for shooting at aircraft. Note that this penetration estimate does not account for ricochet on angled armor. Naval Arcade adds simplified ranging that always aims where the cursor points, lead aids for shells and torpedoes, and a noticeable boost to engine power and traverse for easier handling.

Spotting is generous: detected enemies show on the minimap with markers and nametags listing vehicle name, player nickname, and distance.

Respawns are also forgiving. Ground allows up to 3 ground vehicles per battle, air has no fixed spawn limit (bounded by crew slots and backups, in practice up to around 20), and naval permits up to 3 vessel spawns plus up to 2 aircraft spawns earned with spawn points. Arcade ground also lets you call in temporary aircraft using points from kills and assists.

<Callout type="info">In Arcade ground, a called-in aircraft costs a small number of points by type (roughly 1 for a fighter, 2 for a strike aircraft, 3 for a bomber) and lasts about a minute. A nuclear bomber requires 15 points and is only available at BR 6.0 and above.</Callout>

## Realistic: aim it yourself

Realistic strips out most aids. Air RB removes the lead marker entirely — you aim manually — and enemy aircraft markers are no longer freely shown: visibility depends on range, sun glare, and crew skill rather than a persistent nametag. Ground RB drops the colored crosshair and penetration indicator, and enemy vehicles show no on-screen name tags. Handling gets heavier too: vehicles accelerate slower, bleed far more speed in turns, and turret traverse and gun aiming take longer, with field repairs extended (often up to roughly two minutes). Naval RB keeps a horizontal-only V-shaped lead marker, but it is slow to calculate and the measured range updates only periodically with a real margin of error; secondaries take longer to deploy and refill only at captured points.

Respawns tighten. Standard Air RB is single-spawn: the aircraft you pick is the only one you fly. Ground RB uses a spawn points (SP) system where heavier, more capable vehicles cost more, with extra respawns gated by remaining points and backups.

<Callout type="info">Ground RB can also feature a nuclear bomber as a win condition, but it unlocks at BR 6.7 and above — a higher threshold than the BR 6.0 used in Arcade ground.</Callout>

## Simulator: full manual control

Simulator is first-person from a crew member — commander for ground, pilot for aviation — and ground vehicles use realistic optic positioning rather than barrel-mounted sights. Air SB has no virtual instructor, giving full manual engine and flight control with no automatic error correction, and is tuned for physical peripherals (stick, throttle, pedals). Nothing is marked: enemies must be spotted unaided, even friendly markers fade with distance, and ground vehicles have no aim assistants or lead markers except on radar-equipped vehicles.

Enduring Confrontation (EC) is a separate aircraft mode and the primary format for air Simulator, featuring long battles up to three hours with rooms players can browse and sort by country and rank.

<Callout type="warning">A dedicated standalone naval Simulator mode is not documented in the current Tier-1 wiki pages for game modes; those pages describe Simulator for aviation and ground vehicles. Verify against the live client for Update Heavy Cavalry (2.57) before relying on naval Simulator availability.</Callout>

## Economy at a glance

| Mechanic | Arcade | Realistic | Simulator |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bonus for next vehicle in line | 130% <Term id="RP">RP</Term> | 110% RP | 110% RP |
| Crew XP per 1,000 RP | 30 | 11.3 | 9 |

Per-vehicle <Term id="SL">SL</Term> and RP multipliers, premium account (at least +50% SL, +100% RP), boosters, and talismans are universal mechanics, not mode-specific.

<Callout type="warning">There is no single blanket "RB earns X% more than AB" multiplier, and exact per-vehicle repair costs are patch-dependent. Check each vehicle card in-game rather than citing a fixed figure.</Callout>

## Real-world note

The mode names reflect a design philosophy rather than any historical doctrine: Arcade is a tuning of the game's physics and assists for accessibility, while Realistic and Simulator progressively remove those aids to approach the demands faced by real crews. Treat all of the numeric values above as in-game economy figures only, not statements about historical aircraft, tanks, or ships.
