Winning a gunfight in the air is mostly preparation. Long before the merge, you choose your weapons, load the right belts, and set convergence. This guide covers those decisions as they stand in Update Heavy Cavalry (2.57).

## Machine guns versus cannons

Aircraft guns fall into two broad families. Rifle- and heavy-calibre **machine guns** fire small rounds (7.62 mm, 7.7 mm, .50 cal/12.7 mm, 13.2 mm) at high rate with large ammunition counts. **Autocannons** start around 20 mm and climb to 30 mm, 37 mm, even 45 mm; they fire explosive-filled shells that do far more damage per hit but carry far fewer rounds. The contrast is stark: the F4U-4B's four 20 mm AN/M3 cannons hold roughly 250 rounds each, while the Yak-9K's single 45 mm NS-45 carries just 29.

When an aircraft mixes types, the **weapons selector** lets you fire everything at once or isolate a group. A Spitfire Mk IIb with both 7.7 mm guns and 20 mm cannons can fire them together or save the cannons for the moment you have a clean shot. The active selection is shown by a marker and bound to its own firing key.

## Ammunition belts

Each gun offers several selectable belts, and each belt is a repeating sequence of round types. Common belt names across the wiki are Default, Universal, Air targets, Tracers, Ground targets, and Stealth; some defensive turrets add an Armoured targets belt.

Round abbreviations encode the projectile. Useful ones to recognise:

| Abbrev | Meaning |
|---|---|
| AP / AP-T | armour-piercing (tracer) |
| HE / HEF | high-explosive (fragmentation) |
| I | incendiary |
| T | tracer |
| SAP-I | semi-armour-piercing incendiary |
| <Term id="APHE">APHE</Term> | armour-piercing high-explosive |
| HVAP-T | high-velocity armour-piercing tracer |

Belts differ entirely in what they cycle. For the Attacker FB 1's 20 mm Hispano Mk.V, the Air targets belt runs SAP-I/HEI/AP-T/HEI/SAP-I (explosive-heavy, built to break aircraft), while Ground targets runs AP-T/SAP-I/HEI/AP-T (more penetration for vehicles). The Stealth belt is simply HEI/SAP-I, with no tracers at all.

On larger guns the differences sharpen. The Yak-9K's 45 mm offers single-type belts: HVAP-T has no high-explosive filler and gives that gun's highest penetration, while APHE is armour-piercing with an explosive charge behind it.

### Choosing a belt

- **Universal** is a balanced mix and a common default for air combat.
- **Ground targets** leans armour-piercing for attacking vehicles.
- **Air targets** leans high-explosive/incendiary; on large calibres its fragmentation and overpressure can also hurt trucks, <Term id="SPAA">SPAA</Term>, and emplacements on near misses.
- **Tracers** give visual feedback, sometimes preferred on big guns like 30 mm.
- **Stealth** carries no tracers, so the target gets no warning stream and you can read actual impacts to pick at engines or cockpit. The F4U-4B review calls Stealth its best belt.

<Callout type="tip">Stealth rewards good aim but punishes bad aim: with no tracers to walk onto the target, it is far easier in Arcade (which shows a lead reticle) than in Realistic or Simulator.</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">The wiki's consolidated belt-type overview, including a possible "Night" belt, could not be fetched directly. Treat the full seven-type list as unconfirmed and verify belt names against your aircraft in the current patch.</Callout>

## Convergence

**Convergence** is the distance at which fire from separated guns crosses. Wing-mounted guns angle inward so their rounds meet at the set range; concentrate them at the distance you actually shoot from. Centrally mounted fuselage or belly guns fire straight ahead and need no convergence.

There is no single right number. The J22 page suggests 250-400 m as a solid all-round setting for engaging inside roughly 200-350 m, with 350-600 m for longer shooters and up to 800 m for experts. The Ki-87 page recommends about 500 m. Note that mixed-calibre armament makes very long convergence less reliable, since different calibres drop at different rates.

## Deflection and lead

Rounds take time to reach the target, so you must aim ahead of anything moving. Arcade shows a lead-indicating reticle. Realistic and Simulator do not, so you judge lead yourself from the target's motion and your guns' ballistics, which is exactly why tracerless belts are harder there.

## Bombs and rockets

Bombs and rockets are loadout-selected air-to-ground ordnance. Bombs scale by size and explosive filler; the TBD-1 can carry 100 lb, 500 lb, and 1000 lb general-purpose bombs, plus torpedoes that impose strict drop-speed and drop-altitude limits. Heavier loads are meant for hardened ground targets and bases.

<Callout type="warning">Rocket specifics and bomb behaviour beyond this single example were not deeply confirmed from Tier-1 pages; check your aircraft's loadout details for the current patch.</Callout>

## Real-world note

Many of these guns are historical designs, such as the Hispano cannon series and the Browning M2, whose wartime ammunition belts mixed armour-piercing, incendiary, and tracer rounds for similar reasons. Those real-world details are kept separate from the in-game figures above.
