In air combat, the fight is rarely decided by who turns hardest. It is decided by who manages energy best. Speed and altitude are not separate things in War Thunder's flight model; they are two forms of the same resource, and you spend, store, and trade them throughout an engagement. A pilot who understands this wins fights against aircraft that look superior on paper.

## Energy as a single resource

Treat altitude as stored speed waiting to be cashed in, and speed as altitude you can climb back into. Energy fighting means starting a fight with more of this combined resource than your opponent, or neutralizing their advantage if they begin ahead. Climb rate is the engine of that advantage: a strong climber reaches a height where it sits above most opponents before the merge even happens, banking potential energy for later.

The critical lesson the flight model teaches is *where* energy survives. You retain energy far better in vertical maneuvers than in flat turns. Climbing and diving recycle your energy between its two forms with little loss. Horizontal turning, by contrast, bleeds speed quickly through drag and induced load. Even aircraft built around climb performance lose significant energy in a hard turn, which is exactly why turn-fighting is an expensive choice rather than a free one.

## Boom and Zoom

<Term id="BR">Boom-and-zoom</Term>, or BnZ, is energy fighting at its most direct. You engage from a superior energy state, make one fast diving pass, and extend away instead of committing to a drawn-out fight. The classic platforms are heavy, fast aircraft with weaker maneuverability — the P-47, Fw 190, and Do 335 are textbook examples called out by the official guide.

A few principles make or break the pass:

- **Dive steeper to bank more energy.** A steeper dive converts more altitude into speed, giving you more to spend on the escape afterward. A shallower dive trades some of that speed for a harder-to-counter approach angle, so the choice is situational.
- **Don't fire too early.** Spraying from long range only reveals your position and hands the target time to react. Close the distance, then shoot.
- **Don't zoom climb too soon.** The most common mistake is hauling into a steep climb the instant the pass ends, which throws away the speed you just earned. Keep diving briefly, level out, run straight to bank the energy, *then* climb back up.

The guide recommends holding at least roughly 1 km of altitude advantage over your target, with somewhere around 4,000-4,500 m being a comfortable working altitude for heavier aircraft.

<Callout type="tip">When you climb back up after a pass, you are spending the dive's speed to rebuild altitude. Staying low to turn-fight instead simply wastes that speed and surrenders your advantage.</Callout>

## Knowing when not to chase

Energy discipline is also about restraint. Following a faster-diving opponent straight down tends to burn your own energy for little return, so chasing is often the wrong play. A better option is to keep your height advantage, force an opponent into a costly climb after you, and strike once they slow or stall near the top of that climb. On energy-retaining airframes like the Bearcat and the F4U, such stall traps are a common source of kills.

## Flaps, stalls, and structural limits

Flaps raise turn rate and stall control, but they create drag that bleeds speed, so deploying them is a deliberate energy trade. Do not extend flaps while going vertical and trying to conserve energy. Combat flaps deploy quickly and tolerate higher speeds than takeoff or landing settings, and they help land *stall shots* on an opponent who has departed controlled flight after a reversal.

The flight model also punishes over-speeding. At high speed, control surfaces lose authority and maneuverability degrades — high-speed compression — which is itself a reason to manage energy rather than dive recklessly. Push handling too hard, especially low down, and the aircraft can depart into a flat spin. Good energy retention is what lets you repeat passes and extend safely home afterward.

<Callout type="warning">Exact flap tear-off speeds and structural G/speed limits vary per aircraft and are described here only qualitatively. Verify specific numbers against your aircraft in the current patch (Update Heavy Cavalry, 2.57) before relying on them.</Callout>

## In-game note

The Messerschmitt Bf 109 line is a frequently cited in-game example of an energy fighter, leaning on its climb rate to fight in the vertical rather than in a flat turn. The official guide for it stresses climbing for an altitude advantage before engaging and saving energy through vertical maneuvers. As always, treat any single aircraft's strengths as patch-dependent and check current performance for yourself.
