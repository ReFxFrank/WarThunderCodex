Welcome to the front. War Thunder can feel enormous on day one, but your first hour only needs a few good decisions. This guide gets you into a battle, keeps you oriented, and sets you up to improve.

## Pick a mode first

War Thunder presents its main PvP modes as a difficulty ladder:

| Mode | Feel | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Arcade Battles** | Simplified, fast, forgiving | Day one |
| **Realistic Battles** | More authentic, intermediate | After Arcade clicks |
| **Simulator Battles** | Full immersion, demanding | Later |

Start in **Arcade Battles**. Vehicle control is simplified, and mobility is tuned up to match the mode's quick pace, so machines feel responsive while you learn the fundamentals.

<Callout type="tip">
Arcade carries assists worth leaning on early: spotted enemies appear on your minimap, damaged modules keep working partially instead of dying outright, aircraft cannot be forced into a spin, planes can rearm in flight, and you get multiple respawns.
</Callout>

The aiming help is the headline feature. In **air**, a lead marker shows where to shoot to account for the enemy's path. On the **ground**, an aim assistant projects the impact point and color-codes your penetration chance: red, yellow, or green. At **sea**, you get lead markers for both torpedoes and gunfire to help you place shots ahead of a moving target.

### What changes in Realistic

When you move up, those crutches disappear. There is no air lead marker and no colored ground crosshair telling you where a shot lands. Enemies carry no name tags and are not auto-marked on the minimap. Vehicles also behave more honestly: aircraft have structural limits and can spin or break apart under excessive G-force, and tanks feel heavier, accelerating slowly and bleeding speed in turns. Repairs are stricter too: planes must land on a friendly airfield or carrier to rearm, and a field tank repair can run up to about two minutes. The payoff is higher post-battle rewards.

## Controls and what to rebind

Aircraft in Arcade and Realistic typically use **Mouse Aim**: your mouse sets a desired flight direction rather than directly working the three axes. It is intuitive, and Simulator is the only mode that drops it.

If you use a stick or pad, tune your axes early:

- **Dead zone:** a small one removes stick drift.
- **Nonlinearity:** commonly set in the range of 1 to 2.5 for pitch, roll, and yaw to soften sensitivity near center for finer aim.
- **Throttle:** often benefits from axis inversion so the value rises as you push the stick forward.

Want a button to drive a control instead of an analog axis? Enable **Relative control**, which turns the binding into "increase value" / "decrease value" inputs that persist after you release the key.

<Callout type="info">
Manual prop pitch, mixture, and radiator live under **Full Control** with manual engine control switched on in flight, and not every aircraft exposes those parameters. That is advanced gear you do not need for your first battles, so leave it alone for now.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
Default keybindings for movement and weapon groups vary and were not confirmed from a single source for this writeup, so check your in-game controls menu against the current patch. The confirmed default for **Scouting** is **V**, rebindable under Tank > Miscellaneous.
</Callout>

## Reading the fight and surviving it

Watch the minimap, your ammo, and your damage state. In Arcade the minimap does a lot of work by marking spotted foes; in Realistic you rely far more on your own eyes, with enemy positions depending on your line of sight, the weather, and crew skill.

If you take a light tank of rank II or above (plus a few tank destroyers and certain vehicles armed only with anti-air guided missiles), you can **scout**. In Arcade, lock the target then press scout; in Realistic, place your crosshair on it via binoculars or sniper view, then scout. The target stays marked for 30 seconds, and you earn <Term id="RP">RP</Term> and <Term id="SL">SL</Term>, with a bonus if an ally finishes it inside that window.

Crew skills quietly raise your ceiling as you play. Early priorities: **Weapon Reloading** (loader, cutting ground reload time by up to 23%), **Targeting** (gunner, faster and steadier traverse and elevation), and **Repair Speed**. For pilots, **G-Tolerance** raises the G-force you can take before blacking out and sharpens control accuracy while in Mouse Aim.

Expect to die. Everyone does at first. Spawn, learn one map angle, and try again, multiple respawns in Arcade exist exactly for that.

## Real-world note

The vehicles you are learning are modeled on real machines from roughly the 1930s through the modern era. In-game handling, repair times, and rewards are game balance, not history, so keep those numbers separate from the real aircraft, tanks, and ships they depict.
